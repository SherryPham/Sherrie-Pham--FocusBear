# Inclusive Design Reflection — Working with Vulnerable Populations

## Research & Learn

### Who counts as a vulnerable population, and the challenges they face online
People with ADHD, autism, dyslexia, anxiety, depression, or other mental-health conditions; people with sensory or motor disabilities; older adults; minors; people in financial hardship; people experiencing addiction or recovery. Common digital-space challenges: cognitive overload, dark patterns that exploit impulsivity, inaccessible UI, sensory overwhelm (autoplay video, flashing colours, dense layouts), and tools that assume strong executive function.

### Ethical considerations when designing for neurodivergent users
- Don't exploit attention. Apps for ADHD users especially should not use the same engagement tricks (streaks, infinite scroll, FOMO) that caused the problem in the first place.
- Respect sensory needs — calm visuals, optional sounds, no flashing animations.
- Be transparent. ND users often struggle with hidden mechanics or unpredictable behaviour.
- Build for failure. Missing a day shouldn't punish or shame the user.
- Include lived experience in the design process; don't design at people, design with them.

### Making content more accessible for ADHD/autistic users
- Simple, plain language (avoid idioms, sarcasm, ambiguity).
- Predictable navigation — same things in the same place.
- One clear primary action per screen.
- Short paragraphs, headings, and bullet points over walls of text.
- Visual cues (icons, colour) alongside text.
- Allow customisation (text size, colour, sound, notification frequency).
- Reduce time pressure where possible.

### Supporting neurodivergent team members
- Communicate clearly and directly — avoid hints and read-between-the-lines instructions.
- Provide context: what's the goal, by when, and why does it matter?
- Offer flexibility in working hours and meeting load.
- Use written follow-ups for verbal decisions.
- Respect different processing styles (some people need to think before speaking, some think out loud).
- Don't assume disengagement from quietness or non-eye-contact.

## Reflection

### Adjusting my communication style
Be direct without being blunt — say what I mean, give context for why, and avoid sarcasm or vague phrasing in writing. Put requests and deadlines in writing even after a verbal conversation. Don't read silence or short replies as rudeness or disinterest. Ask people how they prefer to communicate rather than assuming.

### UX/communication pitfalls that could make Focus Bear less accessible
- **Punishing failure** (e.g. visible streak counters that reset to zero) — for ADHD users this can trigger shame spirals and abandonment.
- **Too much text on onboarding screens** — long instructions are exactly what a low-executive-function user can't process.
- **Unclear consequences of actions** — "are you sure?" with no explanation of what changes.
- **Aggressive notifications** — well-intentioned reminders that pile up become another overwhelming inbox.
- **Hidden options buried in menus** — ND users often need fewer, clearer choices, not more flexibility hidden three taps deep.
- **Cheerful/infantilising tone** — being treated like a child trying to do an adult task is a frequent complaint about productivity tools for ND users.

### One practical change I can make
Default to writing things in plain, scannable language with the most important point first — both in user-facing copy and in messages to teammates. Reread anything I write and cut filler before sending.

## Task

### First-person account I read
I read accounts on r/ADHD and the Tiimo blog from late-diagnosed adults describing their experience with productivity tools. The recurring theme: most apps assume the user can already plan, prioritise, and follow through — and then "help" them do it faster. For someone whose actual barrier is starting, those apps just become another thing they feel guilty about not using. The tools that worked were ones that removed decisions rather than added features, and that didn't punish missed days.

### One design/communication improvement Focus Bear could make
Soften streak resets and missed-day language. A streak going from 47 to 0 because of one bad day is a classic shame trigger for ADHD users. A small change — keeping a "best streak" visible, framing a missed day as "rest day" rather than "broken streak", or letting users set their own definition of consistency — would reduce abandonment without removing the motivational benefit of streaks for users who like them.

### A clear, patient, supportive response to a struggling user
> Hi User,
>
> Thanks for writing in — and please don't worry, you haven't done anything wrong.
>
> What you're describing (setting up routines and then not being able to start them) is one of the most common things we hear, and it's not a sign that Focus Bear isn't working for you or that you're failing at it. It's exactly the problem the app is built around, and it can take a few tries to find the setup that clicks.
>
> Two things that often help:
>
> 1. **Make the routine smaller than feels reasonable.** If your morning routine has 8 steps and you keep bouncing off it, try 2 steps for a week. The goal right now is just to start the app, not to have a perfect morning.
> 2. **Pick one anchor habit you already do** (like making coffee) and put one new habit right after it.
>
> No streaks to rebuild, no judgement if today's a write-off. Try whichever of those sounds easier and let me know how it goes — happy to adjust things together.
>
> — Sherrie
